1. Fundamentals of Optical Network

Optical networking is a means of communication that uses signals encoded in light to transmit information in various types of telecommunications networks. These include limited range local-area networks (LAN) or wide-area networks (WAN), which cross metropolitan and regional areas as well as long-distance national, international and transoceanic networks. It is a form of optical communication that relies on optical amplifiers, lasers or LEDs and wave division multiplexing (WDM) to transmit large quantities of data, generally across fiber-optic cables. Because it is capable of achieving extremely high bandwidth, it is an enabling technology for the Internet and telecommunication networks that transmit the vast majority of all human and machine-to-machine information.

The most common fiber-optic networks are communication networks, mesh networks or ring networks commonly used in metropolitan, regional, national and international systems. Another variant of fiber-optic networks is the passive optical network, which uses unpowered optical splitters to link one fiber to multiple premises for last mile applications.

Optical networks have revolutionized global communications by enabling the efficient transmission of optical signals over fiber optic networks. As the demand for high-speed voice and data communication continues to grow, optical networks play a pivotal role in meeting these requirements. This section explores the fundamentals of optical networks and their evolution.

The evolution of fiber optics and the development of optical networks have revolutionized global communications. Optical networks offer high-speed, high-capacity, and reliable transmission, supporting the ever-growing demand for voice and data communication. As technology continues to advance, optical networks will play a crucial role in shaping the future of telecommunications, enabling new applications and possibilities for global connectivity.

2. Network Terminology

Understanding the terminology used in optical networks is essential for grasping the key concepts and operations within this field. Here are some important network terminologies related to optical networks:

Optical Network:
An optical network refers to a communication network that utilizes optical fibers and optical signals for transmitting data.

Optical Fiber:
An optical fiber is a thin, flexible strand made of transparent materials, typically silica-based, used to transmit light signals over long distances with minimal loss.

Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM):
WDM is a technique used in optical networks to multiplex multiple wavelengths of light over a single fiber. It enables the transmission of multiple independent signals simultaneously.

Optical Transmitter:
An optical transmitter is a device that converts electrical signals into optical signals for transmission over optical fibers. It typically uses lasers or light-emitting diodes (LEDs) to generate the optical signals.

Optical Receiver:
An optical receiver is a device that receives optical signals and converts them back into electrical signals for further processing. It typically includes a photodetector to detect the light and convert it into an electrical current.

Optical Amplifier:
An optical amplifier is a device used in optical networks to amplify optical signals without converting them into electrical signals. It helps compensate for signal loss and extend transmission distances.

Optical Switch:
An optical switch is a device that selectively routes optical signals within an optical network. It allows for efficient allocation of bandwidth and enables dynamic reconfiguration of network connections.

Optical Cross-Connect (OXC):
An optical cross-connect is a network element that provides the capability to cross-connect optical signals from various input fibers to desired output fibers. It allows for flexible routing and management of optical traffic.

Optical Add-Drop Multiplexer (OADM):
An OADM is a device used in optical networks to add or drop specific optical channels (wavelengths) from a multiplexed signal. It enables selective extraction or insertion of signals at intermediate network nodes.

Optical Network Unit (ONU):
An ONU is a network element used in passive optical networks (PON) to connect customer premises to the optical network. It receives and transmits optical signals to provide high-speed internet access.

Optical Line Terminal (OLT):
An OLT is a network element used in passive optical networks (PON) to aggregate and manage multiple ONUs. It acts as the central point of control for the optical network.

These terminologies provide a foundational understanding of the key components and operations within optical networks. They are crucial for comprehending the design, implementation, and management of optical communication systems

3. Desirable properties

Optical networks possess several desirable properties that make them highly advantageous for modern communication systems. These properties contribute to their widespread adoption and enable the efficient transmission of large volumes of data over long distances. Here are some of the key desirable properties of optical networks:

High Bandwidth:
Optical networks offer extremely high bandwidth capabilities, enabling the transmission of large amounts of data simultaneously. The wide bandwidth allows for efficient handling of bandwidth-intensive applications such as high-definition video streaming, cloud computing, and data-intensive research.

Long Transmission Distances:
Optical fibers have low signal attenuation, which means they can transmit signals over long distances without significant loss. This property reduces the need for frequent signal regeneration or amplification, resulting in cost-effective network deployments over extensive geographical areas.

Low Signal Degradation:
Optical fibers are less susceptible to external interference and noise, resulting in minimal signal degradation during transmission. This property ensures reliable and high-quality data transmission, making optical networks ideal for applications that require high data integrity and accuracy.

Secure Communication:
Optical networks provide enhanced security for data transmission. Unlike traditional copper-based networks, optical signals are difficult to tap or intercept without causing noticeable signal degradation. This inherent security feature makes optical networks highly suitable for transmitting sensitive and confidential information.

Immunity to Electromagnetic Interference:
Optical fibers are immune to electromagnetic interference (EMI) caused by electrical and radio frequency signals. This property ensures reliable communication even in environments with high levels of electromagnetic noise, such as industrial areas or near power lines.

Scalability:
Optical networks are highly scalable, allowing for easy expansion and increased capacity. The use of wavelength division multiplexing (WDM) technology enables multiple signals to be transmitted over a single fiber, effectively multiplying the network's capacity without the need for additional physical infrastructure.

Energy Efficiency:
Optical networks are more energy-efficient compared to traditional electronic-based networks. Optical signals can be amplified and transmitted over long distances without the need for frequent conversion to electrical signals, resulting in lower power consumption and reduced operational costs.

Flexibility and Reconfigurability:
Optical networks offer flexibility and reconfigurability in terms of network topology and connections. Optical switches and cross-connects allow for dynamic rerouting of optical signals, enabling efficient resource utilization and adaptability to changing network demands.

Compatibility with Existing Infrastructure:
Optical networks can seamlessly integrate with existing communication infrastructure. They can coexist with traditional copper-based networks, facilitating smooth migration and upgrades to higher-speed optical technologies without disrupting existing services.

Future-Proofing:
Optical networks provide a future-proof solution for ever-increasing bandwidth demands. Advancements in fiber technology and transmission techniques continue to push the limits of data transmission speeds, ensuring that optical networks can support the evolving needs of communication systems.

These desirable properties of optical networks make them a critical and reliable technology for meeting the growing demands of high-speed, high-capacity communication in various sectors, including telecommunications, internet services, enterprise networks, and research institutions.

4. Elements of an Optical Network



Fig. 5.1: Elements of an Optical Network


Stations:
Stations in an optical network serves as the source and destination of the information being transmitted and received. Stations are basically those devices that are used by the users of the network. For example, a computer or any other telecommunication device.

Trunk:
A trunk is basically a transmission line i.e., optical fiber cable in order to transmit the optical signal. A network is composed of one or multiple trunks for signal transmission over large distance.

Node:
Node is nothing but acts as a hub for multiple transmission lines inside the network. In case of a single transmission line, an optical network does not require nodes, as in this case stations at both the ends can be directly connected to the fiber cables.

Topology:
When multiple fiber cables are employed in an optical network, then these are connected through nodes. But the way in which the multiple nodes are connected together denotes the topology of the network.

Router:
A router is basically placed inside an optical network that provides a suitable path for signal transmission.

5. Optical Network Topology Types

In optical networks, the arrangement of multiple optical fiber transmission lines is known as topology. Let's explore the different configurations of topology:



Fig. 5.2: Optical Network Topology Types



Bus Topology:
In a bus topology, nodes are connected through a single trunk line using optical couplers. This configuration provides a cost-effective method for signal transmission. However, identifying a faulted node and restoring the signal can be challenging in this topology.



Fig. 5.3: Bus Topology



Ring Topology:
In a ring topology, nodes are connected in a closed path where each node is linked to its neighboring node. The transmitted light signals travel from one node to another. Optical couplers are used to facilitate signal coupling between nodes within the network.



Fig. 5.4: Ring Topology



Star Topology:
In a star topology, nodes are connected to a central hub, which can be an active or passive network component. The central hub controls and directs the transmitted optical signals within the network, enhancing signal management.



Fig. 5.5: Star Topology



Mesh Topology:
In a mesh topology, nodes are interconnected in an arbitrary manner, forming point-to-point connections. This configuration offers flexibility, allowing for dynamic signal routing based on the application. In case of a failure in one node, alternative paths can be used for signal transmission. In a mesh topology, the failed link or node is detected, and the signal traffic is redirected to an alternate link within the connection.



Fig. 5.6: Mesh Topology

FDDI

FDDI stands for Fiber Distributed Data Interface. It is a high-speed, high-bandwidth network based on optical transmissions. It is most often used as a network backbone, for connecting high-end computers (mainframes, minicomputers, and peripherals), and for LANs connecting high-performance engineering, graphics, and other workstations that demand a rapid transfer of large amounts of data. It can transport data at a rate of 100 Megabits per second and can support up to 500 stations on a single network. FDDI was designed to run through fiber cables, transmitting light pulses to convey information between stations, but it can also run on copper using electrical signals. It is relatively expensive to implement, although the mixing of fiber-optic with copper cabling can hold down the cost.

FDDI is highly reliable because FDDI networks consist of two counter-rotating rings. A secondary ring provides an alternate data path in the event a fault occurring on the primary ring. FDDI stations incorporate this secondary ring into the data path to route traffic around the fault. FDDI based on a ring topology with token passing. It is an advanced technology, in the form of the token ring over optical fiber. FDDI developed for two primary reasons: to support and help extend the capabilities of older LANs, such as Ethernet and Token Ring, and to provide a reliable infrastructure for businesses, moving even mission-critical applications to networks.

Although FDDI is not really a WAN technology (its ring are limited to a maximum length of 100 kilometers or 62 miles), the ground it can cover does make it suitable for use as a backbone, connecting a number of smaller LANs, and it can provide the core of a network as extensive as a Metropolitan Area Network (MAN). In that sense, FDDI is more than LAN but less than WAN. Also, because FDDI transfers information extremely quickly (l00 Mbps), it is often used to connect high-end devices such as mainframes, minicomputers, and peripherals, or to connect high-performance devices within a LAN. Engineering or video or graphics workstations, for instance, benefit from FODI because they need much bandwidth, to transfer large amounts of data at satisfactorily high steeds. FDDI can easily be incorporated into existing networks because it works even with other network topologies (due to the LLC protocol that is also used by Ethernet and Token Ring).



Fig. 5.7: FDDI



FDDI offers the following features:
  1. High transmission rates (100 Mbps) and bandwidth.
  2. Real throughput rate (20 stations expected) of approx. 95 Mbps.
  3. Large extensions (max. 100 km).
  4. Great node-to-node distance (2km using multimode fiber, 40 km using single mode fiber).
  5. Available for both fiber and copper media.
  6. Easier to maintain.
  7. Compatible to standards-based components and various operating systems.

FTTx

Fibre to the X is a collective term that is used to describe various types of broadband network architectures, depending on wherever they terminate. The ‘X’ in ‘FTTx’ represents a particular object. It could be a home, a cabinet or any end-user premise. Resultantly, FTTx could be Fibre to the Home (FTTH), Fibre to the Building (FTTB), Fibre to the Premises (FTTP) and Fibre to the Curb (FTTC). FTTx is used to drive next-generation access by a significant upgrade to the broadband available by making a step-change in the speed and quality of the service.

Fibre to the X (FTTx) network architecture finds its use in Last-mile connectivity. The network is spread out from the end-user premise to the carrier network edge. It can deliver faster and better connectivity to homes and enterprises around the world.

FTTx has many benefits related to speed and capacity and that is the reason legacy copper-based networks are being replaced with Fibre to the X (FTTx). Other advantages include higher transmission rates and lower energy consumption. Fibre to the X network takes fibre closer to the end-user. This helps in leveraging the latest construction, connection and transmission techniques.

With the development of cloud computing, smart cities and 5G, requirements for higher bandwidth and network speed have increased. Fibre to the X offers a low-latency, high-bandwidth fibre network that can fulfil all these requirements. In addition, it also helps in achieving high capacity and consistent connectivity. It also helps in providing long-distance signal transmission, a lightweight form factor, and immunity against electromagnetic interference.



Fig. 5.8: FTTx



FTTP
FTTP, or Fiber To The Premises, is a broadband network architecture that brings fiber-optic cables directly to the end-user's premises. It offers ultra-fast and reliable internet connectivity, delivering symmetrical high-speed data transmission for both uploads and downloads. With gigabit speeds and low latency, FTTP enables seamless streaming, online gaming, video conferencing, and other data-intensive applications. The use of fiber optics ensures enhanced reliability, scalability, and future-proofing, accommodating increasing bandwidth demands. FTTP eliminates distance limitations and offers a stable connection, even over long distances. While it requires significant infrastructure investment, FTTP provides a superior internet experience, empowering homes and businesses with high-performance broadband connectivity.

FTTH
FTTH, or Fiber To The Home, is a broadband network architecture that brings fiber-optic cables directly to individual residences. It provides high-speed and reliable internet access, enabling fast downloads, seamless streaming, and real-time online activities. With FTTH, users experience symmetrical bandwidth, allowing for equally fast uploads and downloads. The use of fiber optics ensures enhanced reliability, stability, and future-proofing, accommodating increasing data demands. FTTH eliminates distance limitations, providing consistent performance across the coverage area. While it requires a significant investment in fiber infrastructure, FTTH delivers an exceptional internet experience, empowering households with superior connectivity for a wide range of applications and services.

PON

Passive Optical Network (PON) is a fiber-optic network architecture widely used in telecommunications to provide high-speed broadband services to multiple subscribers. It offers an efficient and cost-effective solution for delivering data, voice, and video services. Let's delve into PON in more detail:

Architecture:
Optical Line Terminal (OLT): The OLT is the central hub located at the service provider's central office. It connects to the service provider's backbone network and manages the distribution of data to the subscribers.

Optical Network Units (ONUs): ONUs are installed at the subscriber's premises and serve as the endpoints of the PON. They connect to the user's devices, such as computers, phones, or set-top boxes.



Fig. 5.9: PON



Passive Splitting:
PON utilizes passive optical splitters to divide the optical signal from the OLT into multiple paths that reach the ONUs. These splitters require no power and are capable of splitting the signal to serve numerous subscribers.

The most common type of splitter used in PON is the 1xN splitter, where N represents the number of output paths.

Upstream and Downstream Transmission:
PON supports both downstream and upstream transmission. In the downstream direction, the OLT sends data to the ONUs, while in the upstream direction, the ONUs transmit data back to the OLT.

Time Division Multiplexing (TDM) or Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM) techniques are used to share the available bandwidth among multiple subscribers.

PON Technologies:
1. GPON (Gigabit PON):
GPON offers high-speed transmission and is widely deployed in PON networks. It provides downstream speeds of up to 2.5 Gbps and upstream speeds of up to 1.25 Gbps.

2. EPON (Ethernet PON):
EPON is based on Ethernet standards and provides symmetrical speeds of up to 1 Gbps for both downstream and upstream transmission.

Advantages of PON:
1. Cost Efficiency: PON networks reduce infrastructure costs by utilizing passive optical splitters, which eliminate the need for active electronic components in the distribution network.

2. Scalability: PON networks are highly scalable, allowing service providers to add new subscribers without significant infrastructure upgrades.

3. High Bandwidth: PON provides high-speed broadband connectivity, offering faster download and upload speeds compared to traditional copper-based networks.

4. Reliability: PON networks are known for their reliability, as fiber-optic cables are less susceptible to electromagnetic interference and signal degradation.

Applications:
1. PON is widely used in residential areas, office buildings, and multi-dwelling units to deliver high-speed internet, voice services, and video content.

2. PON is also used in smart city initiatives, enabling efficient communication and connectivity for various applications like traffic management, public safety, and environmental monitoring.

3. PON technology continues to evolve, with advancements like XGS-PON and 10G-EPON, which offer higher speeds to meet the growing demands of bandwidth-intensive applications. Overall, PON provides a robust and scalable solution for delivering high-speed broadband services to a large number of subscribers in a cost-effective manner.

GPON

GPON (Gigabit Passive Optical Network) is a specific type of passive optical network technology widely used in telecommunications for high-speed broadband services. It is a fiber-optic network architecture that enables the efficient transmission of data, voice, and video signals.

Architecture:
Optical Line Terminal (OLT): The OLT serves as the central hub in the GPON network, located at the service provider's central office. It interfaces with the service provider's backbone network and manages the distribution of data to the subscriber ONUs.

Optical Network Unit (ONU): The ONU is the endpoint device installed at the subscriber's premises. It connects to the user's devices, such as computers, phones, or set-top boxes.



Fig. 5.10: GPON



Downstream and Upstream Transmission:
1. In GPON, the downstream channel carries data from the OLT to the ONUs. It supports high-speed transmission, typically up to 2.5 Gbps.

2. The upstream channel allows data transmission from the ONUs back to the OLT, typically up to 1.25 Gbps. This enables bidirectional communication between the service provider and the subscriber.

Splitting Ratio:
GPON supports a high splitting ratio, allowing a single OLT port to serve multiple ONUs. The most common splitting ratios are 1:32 or 1:64, which means that one OLT port can connect to 32 or 64 ONUs, respectively.

TDM and WDM Technology:
GPON utilizes a combination of Time Division Multiplexing (TDM) and Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM) techniques for efficient bandwidth allocation. TDM divides the available bandwidth into time slots, allowing multiple ONUs to share the same physical fiber. WDM separates different wavelengths of light to enable multiple channels of data transmission.

Triple Play Services:
GPON supports the delivery of triple play services, which include high-speed internet, voice, and video services over a single fiber-optic connection. This enables service providers to offer bundled packages to subscribers, enhancing their user experience.

Advantages of GPON:
1. High Bandwidth: GPON provides gigabit-level downstream and upstream speeds, meeting the increasing demand for high-speed internet access and multimedia applications.

2. Cost Efficiency: GPON networks are cost-effective due to the use of passive optical splitters, which reduce the need for active electronic components.

3. Scalability: GPON networks are highly scalable, allowing service providers to add more subscribers without significant infrastructure upgrades.

4. Fiber Reach: GPON can support long-distance fiber connections, extending up to 20 kilometers or more.

GPON has become a widely adopted technology for delivering high-speed broadband services to residential, business, and institutional users. Its efficient use of fiber optics and high capacity make it a reliable and future-proof solution for meeting the growing demands of modern communication networks.

Long Haul

In the context of telecommunications, "long haul" refers to the transmission of data or signals over a significant distance, typically spanning hundreds or thousands of kilometers. It is commonly used to describe the transport of information between distant locations or across regions/countries.

To achieve long-haul transmission, advanced technologies and techniques are employed to ensure signal integrity, minimize signal degradation, and maximize data throughput. These may include:

- Dense wavelength division multiplexing (DWDM): DWDM enables multiple signals of different wavelengths (colors) to be transmitted over a single fiber, significantly increasing the capacity and efficiency of long-haul networks.

- Optical amplification: Optical amplifiers, such as erbium-doped fiber amplifiers (EDFAs), are used to boost optical signals periodically along the fiber-optic transmission path, compensating for signal losses and extending the reach of the network.

- Forward error correction (FEC): FEC techniques are implemented to enhance the reliability of data transmission by adding redundant information that allows the receiver to correct errors in the received signal.

Long-haul networks require careful planning, extensive infrastructure, and maintenance to ensure optimal performance and uninterrupted connectivity over extended distances. They form the backbone of global communications, facilitating seamless data transfer and enabling worldwide connectivity.

Long-haul communication is essential for various applications, including:

- Interconnecting data centers: Data centers located in different geographic regions need to exchange large amounts of data reliably and quickly. Long-haul networks facilitate this data transfer, enabling efficient cloud computing, content delivery, and disaster recovery.

- Telecommunications backbone: Long-haul networks form the backbone of telecommunications infrastructure, connecting central offices, exchanges, and switching centers across vast distances. These networks carry voice, data, and video traffic between different regions and countries.

- Internet connectivity: Long-haul connections play a crucial role in linking internet service providers (ISPs) and connecting users to global internet exchange points (IXPs). They ensure reliable and high-speed internet access for users, regardless of their geographic location.

- Submarine cables: Long-haul connections are vital for submarine fiber optic cables that span across oceans, connecting continents and enabling international data and voice communications.

Metro

In the context of optical fiber communications, "metro" refers to metropolitan or metropolitan area networks (MANs). A metro network is a high-speed, high-capacity optical fiber network that spans a metropolitan area or a city. It connects various locations within the city, such as office buildings, data centers, campuses, and residential areas, to provide fast and reliable communication services.

Metro networks serve as the middle layer between long-haul networks that span across larger distances and local area networks (LANs) within individual buildings or campuses. They play a crucial role in facilitating efficient data transmission and connectivity within urban areas.

Key features and characteristics of metro networks include:

Geographic coverage: Metro networks cover a specific metropolitan area or city, serving as a local infrastructure for interconnecting different locations and users within the urban environment.

High bandwidth capacity: Metro networks are designed to handle large volumes of data traffic, supporting high-speed data transfer, voice, video, and other bandwidth-intensive applications.

Low latency and high reliability: Metro networks are optimized for low latency, ensuring minimal delays in data transmission. They also incorporate redundancy and fault-tolerant features to maintain high reliability and minimize network downtime.

Fiber optic infrastructure: Metro networks primarily utilize fiber optic cables for their high bandwidth capabilities and low signal attenuation, allowing for long-distance transmission within the metropolitan area.

Multi-service support Metro networks support various communication services, including internet connectivity, virtual private networks (VPNs), cloud services, voice over IP (VoIP), video streaming, and other enterprise and residential applications.

Scalability and flexibility: Metro networks are designed to accommodate future growth and increased bandwidth demands. They can be easily expanded or upgraded to support additional users, locations, and services.

Metro networks play a crucial role in enabling efficient communication and connectivity within cities, supporting businesses, institutions, and individuals with fast and reliable data transfer. They are essential for powering urban economies, enabling smart city initiatives, and providing seamless connectivity for a wide range of applications and services.

Access

An optical access network is a type of telecommunications network that utilizes optical fiber technology to provide high-speed connectivity and services to end-users. It is designed to deliver data, voice, and video services from the service provider's network to individual homes, businesses, or institutions.

Optical access network (OAN) refers to the use of optical fiber as the main transmission medium to realize the information transmission function of the access network. It is connected to the service node through an optical line terminal (OLT) and connected to the user through an optical network unit (ONU). The optical access network includes remote devices – optical network units and central office devices – optical line terminals that are connected by transmission devices.

The role of the OLT is to provide an interface between the access network and the local switch, and communicate with the optical network unit of the client through optical transmission. It completely separates the switching function of the switch from user access. The optical line terminal provides maintenance and monitoring for itself and the user. It can be placed directly at the exchange or with the local switch at the remote end.

The role of the ONU is to provide a user-side interface to the access network. It can be connected to a variety of user terminals, with photoelectric conversion and corresponding maintenance and monitoring functions. The main function of the ONU is to terminate the fiber from the OLT, process the optical signal and provide business interfaces for multiple small businesses, business users and residential users. The network side of the ONU is an optical interface, and the user end is an electrical interface. Therefore, the ONU has optical/electrical and electrical/optical conversion functions. It also has the digital/analog and analog/digital conversion functions of the dialogue. The ONU is usually placed closer to the user and has a great deal of flexibility.

The main features of the optical access network
1. The network coverage radius is generally small, and no repeater can be needed. However, due to the sharing of optical power or wavelength distribution caused by many users sharing optical fibers, it may be necessary to use a fiber amplifier for power compensation.
2. It is required to meet the transmission of various broadband services, and the transmission quality is good and the reliability is high.
3. The application range of the fiber access network is broad.
4. The investment cost is large, the network management is complicated, and the remote power supply is difficult
