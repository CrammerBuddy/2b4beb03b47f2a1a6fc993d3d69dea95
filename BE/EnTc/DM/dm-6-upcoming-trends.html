1. Introduction

Photodetectors are electronic devices that are designed to detect and convert light or optical signals into an electrical signal. They play a crucial role in various applications, including telecommunications, imaging systems, optical data storage, sensing, and many others. The fundamental principle behind photodetectors is the ability to convert photons, which are particles of light, into measurable electrical current or voltage.

Photodetectors are based on the phenomenon known as the photoelectric effect, which was first described by Albert Einstein in 1905. According to the photoelectric effect, when light interacts with certain materials, it can cause the emission of electrons. This effect forms the basis for the operation of photodetectors.

The key components of a typical photodetector include a photosensitive material, a p-n junction or other types of semiconductor structures, and electrical contacts. The photosensitive material, often a semiconductor material such as silicon or germanium, absorbs incident light and generates electron-hole pairs. The p-n junction or semiconductor structure serves as a means to separate and collect these electron-hole pairs.

When photons strike the surface of the photosensitive material, they transfer their energy to electrons, allowing them to break free from their atomic bonds and become free carriers. These liberated carriers can then be collected as an electrical current or induce a voltage across the device, which can be measured and used for various applications.

Photodetectors can be categorized into several types based on their working principles and materials used. Some common types include:

1. Photodiodes:
These are the most widely used photodetectors. They are based on p-n junctions and operate in reverse bias. When photons are absorbed by the depletion region of the junction, electron-hole pairs are generated, leading to a current flow.
2. Phototransistors:
These devices are similar to photodiodes but incorporate an additional amplification stage. They offer higher sensitivity compared to photodiodes and are often used in low-light conditions.
3. Avalanche Photodiode (APD):
APDs are specialized photodetectors that utilize the avalanche effect to achieve higher levels of sensitivity. The avalanche effect is a phenomenon where carriers, generated by absorbed photons, can trigger a multiplication process, resulting in a higher current gain.

The performance of a photodetector is characterized by various parameters, including responsivity (the ratio of output current or voltage to incident power), quantum efficiency (the ratio of generated carriers to incident photons), response time, noise characteristics, and spectral range.

So, the photodetectors are essential devices for detecting and converting optical signals into electrical signals. They find wide-ranging applications in fields such as telecommunications, imaging, sensing, and more. Understanding the principles and operation of different types of photodetectors is crucial for harnessing their capabilities effectively.

2. Requirements from Photodetectors

The optical signal is weakened and distorted when it hits the detector. Therefore, the photodetector must meet very high performance requirements such as:

Sensitivity:
Optical detectors must be sensitive to the specific wavelength of light for which they are designed.

High Responsitivity:
It refers to the ability of an optical detector to convert incident photons into an electrical signal. Measured in amps per watt.

Linearity:
Optical detectors must exhibit a linear response to changes in light intensity.

Stability:
Optical detectors must be stable over time and under changing environmental conditions.

Noise free operation.

Long operational life.

High conversion efficiency or high response.

Short transit time.

3. Material Considerations

The material used in the design of photodetectors is a significant concern since it can have a substantial impact on their performance characteristics. Some of the most important material considerations for photodetectors are as follows:

Bandgap energy:
The bandgap energy of the material used in the photodetector determines the wavelength of light that can be detected. Materials with larger bandgap energies can detect shorter-wavelength light, while materials with smaller bandgap energies can detect longer-wavelength light.

Responsivity:
The responsivity of a photodetector is a measure of how efficiently it converts incident light into an electrical signal. Materials with high responsivity are desirable for applications that require high sensitivity.

Dark Current:
The dark current of a photodetector is the current that flows through it in the absence of light. Materials with low dark current are desirable for applications that require low noise levels.

Speed:
The speed of a photodetector is determined by the material's carrier mobility and lifetime. Materials with high carrier mobility and short carrier lifetimes are desirable for high-speed applications.

Stability:
The stability of a photodetector is determined by the material's resistance to aging and degradation. Materials with high stability are desirable for applications that require long-term reliability.

Cost:
The cost of the material used in a photodetector can be an important consideration, particularly for mass-produced devices. Materials that are readily available and inexpensive are often preferred.

Some common materials used in photodetectors include silicon, germanium, gallium arsenide, indium gallium arsenide, and mercury cadmium telluride. The choice of material depends on the specific application requirements and performance characteristics desired.

4. Photodiode

A photodiode is a p-n junction or pin semiconductor device that consumes light energy to generate electric current. It is also sometimes referred to as a photo-detector, photo-sensor, or light detector.

Photodiodes are specially designed to operate in reverse bias condition. Reverse bias means that the p-side of the photodiode is connected to the negative terminal of the battery and n-side is connected to the positive terminal of the battery.

Photodiode is very sensitive to light so when light or photons fall on the photodiode it easily converts light into electric current. Solar cell is also known as a large area photodiode because it converts solar energy or light energy into electric energy. However, solar cells work only in bright light.

The symbol of the photodiode is similar to the normal p-n junction diode except that it contains arrows striking the diode. The arrows striking the diode represent light or photons.

4.2. How does a photodiode work?
A normal P-N junction diode allows a small amount of electric current under reverse bias condition. To increase the electric current under reverse bias conditions, we need to generate more minority carriers.

The external reverse voltage applied to the P-N junction diode will supply energy to the minority carriers but not increase the population of minority carriers.

However, a small number of minority carriers are generated due to external reverse bias voltage. The minority carriers generated at N-side or P-side will recombine in the same material before they cross the junction. As a result, no electric current flows due to these charge carriers. For example, the minority carriers generated in the P-type material experience a repulsive force from the external voltage and try to move towards the N-side. However, before crossing the junction, the free electrons recombine with the holes within the same material. As a result, no electric current flows.

To overcome this problem, we need to apply external energy directly to the depletion region to generate more charge carriers.

A special type of diode called photodiode is designed to generate more charge carriers in depletion regions. In photodiodes, we use light or photons as the external energy to generate charge carriers in depletion regions.

4.3. Photodiode Operation Modes
A photodiode can be operated in one of the two modes: photovoltaic mode or photoconductive mode. Operation mode selection of the photodiode depends upon the speed requirements of the application and the amount of dark current that is tolerable.

-Photovoltaic Mode:
In the photovoltaic mode, the photodiode is unbiased. In other words, no external voltage is applied to the photodiode under photovoltaic mode.

In photovoltaic mode, dark current is very low. Photodiodes operated in photovoltaic mode have low response speed.

The photodiodes operated in photovoltaic mode are generally used for low speed applications or for detecting low light levels.

-Photoconductive Mode:
In photoconductive mode, an external reverse bias voltage is applied to the photodiode.

Applying a reverse bias voltage increases the width of the depletion region and reduces the junction capacitance which results in increased response speed. The reverse bias also increases the dark current.

Photodiodes operated in photoconductive mode have high noise current. This is due to the reverse saturation current flowing through the photodiode.

-Avalanche Mode:
In avalanche mode, the photodiode is connected with high reverse bias voltage. The high reverse voltage increases the depletion width and the potential across it. Due to high potential, the electron-hole pair flows with maximum speed hitting more atoms in its path resulting in more photocurrent. Therefore, avalanche mode has high internal gain and responsivity.

4.5. Types of Photodiodes
The working operation of all types of photodiodes is the same. Different types of photodiodes are developed based on specific applications. For example, PIN photodiodes are developed to increase the response speed. PIN photodiodes are used where high response speed is needed.

The different types of photodiodes are:

A. P-N Junction Photodiode:
PN junction photodiodes are the first form of photodiodes. They are the most widely used photodiodes before the development of PIN photodiodes. PN junction photodiode is also simply referred as photodiode. Nowadays, PN junction photodiodes are not widely used.

When external light energy is supplied to the p-n junction photodiode, the valence electrons in the depletion region gain energy.

If the light energy applied to the photodiode is greater the band-gap of semiconductor material, the valence electrons gain enough energy and break bonding with the parent atom. The valence electron which breaks bonding with the parent atom will become a free electron. Free electrons move freely from one place to another place by carrying the electric current.



Fig. 3.3: P-N Junction Photodiode


When the valence electron leaves the valence shell an empty space is created in the valence shell at which the valence electron left. This empty space in the valence shell is called a hole. Thus, both free electrons and holes are generated as pairs. The mechanism of generating electron-hole pair by using light energy is known as the inner photoelectric effect.

The minority carriers in the depletion region experience force due to the depletion region electric field and the external electric field. For example, free electrons in the depletion region experience repulsive and attractive force from the negative and positive ions present at the edge of depletion region at p-side and n-side. As a result, free electrons move towards the N-region. When the free electrons reaches n region, they are attracted towards the positive terminals of the battery. In the similar way, holes move in opposite direction.

The strong depletion region electric field and the external electric field increase the drift velocity of the free electrons. Because of this high drift velocity, the minority carriers (free electrons and holes) generated in the depletion region will cross the p-n junction before they recombine with atoms. As a result, the minority carrier current increases.

When no light is applied to the reverse bias photodiode, it carries a small reverse current due to external voltage. This small electric current under the absence of light is called dark current. It is denoted by Iλ.

In a photodiode, reverse current is independent of reverse bias voltage. Reverse current is mostly depends on the light intensity.

In photodiodes, most of the electric current is carried by the charge carriers generated in the depletion region because the charge carriers in depletion region has high drift velocity and low recombination rate whereas the charge carriers in n-side or p-side has low drift velocity and high recombination rate. The electric current generated in the photodiode due to the application of light is called photocurrent.

The total current through the photodiode is the sum of the dark current and the photocurrent. The dark current must be reduced to increase the sensitivity of the device.

The electric current flowing through a photodiode is directly proportional to the incident number of photons.

B. PIN Photodiode:
PIN photodiodes are developed from the PN junction photodiodes. The operation of PIN photodiode is similar to the PN junction photodiode except that the PIN photodiode is manufactured differently to improve its performance.

The PIN photodiode is developed to increase the minority carrier current and response speed.

PIN photodiodes generate more electric current than the PN junction photodiodes with the same amount of light energy.

-Layers of PIN Photodiode:
In PIN photodiode, an addition layer called intrinsic semiconductor is placed between the P-type and n-type semiconductor to increase the minority carrier current.

A PIN photodiode is made of p region and n region separated by a highly resistive intrinsic layer. The intrinsic layer is placed between the p region and n region to increase the width of depletion region.

The p-type and n-type semiconductors are heavily doped. Therefore, the p region and n region of the PIN photodiode has large number of charge carriers to carry electric current. However, these charge carriers will not carry electric current under reverse bias condition.

On the other hand, intrinsic semiconductor is an undoped semiconductor material. Therefore, the intrinsic region does not have charge carriers to conduct electric current.

Under reverse bias condition, the majority charge carriers in n region and p region moves away from the junction. As a result, the width of depletion region becomes very wide. Therefore, majority carriers will not carry electric current under reverse bias condition.

However, the minority carriers will carry electric current because they experience repulsive force from the external electric field.

When light or photon energy is applied to the PIN diode, most part of the energy is observed by the intrinsic or depletion region because of the wide depletion width. As a result, a large number of electron-hole pairs are generated.

Due to the electric field applied to pin photodiode the free electrons generated in the intrinsic region move towards n-side whereas holes generated in the intrinsic region move towards p-side which are attracted to towards the positive and negative terminals of the battery.

This flow of electron generates electric current thus converting photon or light energy into electric energy.



Fig. 3.4: PIN Photodiode


NOTE: The population of minority carriers in PIN photodiode is very large compared to the PN junction photodiode. Therefore, PIN photodiode carry large minority carrier current than PN junction photodiode.

When forward bias voltage is applied to the PIN photodiode, it behaves like a resistor.

We know that capacitance is directly proportional to the size of electrodes and inversely proportional to the distance between electrodes. In PIN photodiode, the p region and n region acts as electrodes and intrinsic region acts as dielectric.

The separation distance between p region and n region in PIN photodiode is very large because of the wide depletion width. Therefore, PIN photodiode has low capacitance compared to the PN junction photodiode.

In PIN photodiode, most of the electric current is carried by the charge carriers generated in the depletion region. The charge carriers generated in p region or n region carry only a small electric current. Therefore, increasing the width of depletion region increases the minority carrier electric current.

-Advantages:
-Wide bandwidth.
-High Quantum Efficiency.
-High Response Speed.

C. Avalanche Photodiode:
The diode which uses the avalanche method to provide extra performance as compared to other diodes is known as avalanche photodiode.

These diodes are used to change the signals from optical to electrical. These diodes can be operated in high reverse bias. The avalanche photodiode symbol is similar to the Zener diode.



Fig. 3.5: Avalanche Photodiode Symbol


-Construction
The construction of both the PIN photodiode and Avalanche photodiode is similar. This diode includes two heavily doped & two lightly doped regions. Here, heavily doped regions are P+ & N+ whereas lightly doped regions are I & P.

In the intrinsic region, the depletion layer width is fairly thinner in this diode as compared to the PIN photodiode. Here, the p+ region works like the anode whereas the n+ region acts as the cathode.

As compared to other photodiodes, this diode works in a high reverse bias condition. So this allows avalanche multiplication of the charge carriers formed through the light impact or photon. The avalanche action allows the gain of the photodiode to be enhanced several times to provide a high range of sensitivity.

-Working Principle and Operation
Avalanche breakdown occurs mainly once the photodiode is subjected to maximum reverse voltage. This voltage enhances the electric field beyond the depletion layer. When incident light penetrates the p+ region then it gets absorbed within the extremely resistive p region then electron-hole pairs are generated.

Charge carriers drift including their saturation velocity to the PN+ region wherever a high electric field exists. When the velocity is highest, then charge carriers will collide through other atoms & produce new electron-hole pairs. A huge charge carrier’s pair will result in high photocurrent.

This diode operation can be done in a depleted mode completely. However, they can also work in the Geiger mode in addition to the linear avalanche mode. In this type of operation mode, the photodiode can be operated at the above breakdown voltage. At present, another mode is launched namely “Sub-Geiger mode”.

-Avalanche Photodiode Characterstics
Avalanche photodiodes are highly sensitive, high-speed-based diodes which use an internal gain method that works through applying a reverse voltage. As compared to PIN type photodiode, these diodes measure low range light so used in different applications where high sensitivity is required like measurement of optical distance and optical communication for long-distance.

There are different avalanche photodiode families which are designed mainly for detecting short wavelengths otherwise near-infrared.

5. Photo Transistor

The photodiode and phototransistor both convert the light energy into the electrical energy. A phototransistor is a three-layer semiconductor device with a light-sensitive base region that converts light into current flowing between the collector and emitter regions.But phototransistor is mostly preferred over the photodiode because of their following advantages.

-The current gain in the phototransistor is more than the phototransistor even if the same amount of light strike on it.
-The sensitivity of the phototransistor is higher than the photodiode.
-The photodiode can be converted into the phototransistor by removing their emitter terminals.

The response time of the photodiode is much higher than the phototransistor. The output current of the photodiode is in microamperes, and it can switch on or off in nanoseconds. While the response time of the phototransistor is in microseconds and it provides current in milliamperes.

It is similar to an ordinary transistor, except it lacks a base terminal. Instead of base current, it uses light energy as input.

5.2. Working:
The phototransistor is a semiconductor device composed of semiconductor material. 6. Quantum efficiency

Quantum efficiency (QE) is a key performance parameter used to quantify the efficiency of a photodetector in converting incident photons into electric current. It represents the ratio of the number of electron-hole pairs generated by absorbed photons to the total number of incident photons.

Mathematically, quantum efficiency (QE) is expressed as:

QE = (Number of electron-hole pairs generated) / (Number of incident photons)

A high quantum efficiency indicates that a larger proportion of incident photons are successfully converted into electron-hole pairs, resulting in a stronger photocurrent.

The quantum efficiency of a photodetector is influenced by various factors, including the absorption properties of the material, the bandgap energy, and the design of the detector.

Quantum efficiency is an essential parameter to consider when selecting or designing a photodetector for specific applications. A higher quantum efficiency translates to a more sensitive and efficient photodetector, making it suitable for applications where low light levels or high detection accuracy are required, such as imaging, spectroscopy, and telecommunications.

7. Responsivity

Responsivity is another important parameter used to characterize the performance of a photodetector. It represents the electrical output (typically current or voltage) produced by the photodetector for a given amount of incident optical power.

Mathematically, responsivity (R) is expressed as:

R = (Electrical output) / (Incident optical power)

Responsivity is typically measured in units of Ampere per Watt (A/W) or Volts per Watt (V/W), indicating the amount of electrical output generated per unit of incident optical power.

Responsivity takes into account the efficiency of the photodetector in converting incident optical power into an electrical signal. It considers both the quantum efficiency (QE) of the photodetector and the collection efficiency of the generated carriers.

9. Detector Response Time

Detector response time, also referred to as the rise time or the time response, is a crucial parameter that characterizes the speed at which a photodetector can respond to changes in incident light intensity. It quantifies how quickly the photodetector can convert the optical input signal into an electrical output signal.

The response time of a photodetector is primarily determined by the intrinsic properties of the detector and the dynamics of the charge carriers generated by the incident light.

The response time is typically measured as the time required for the photodetector's electrical output signal to reach a certain percentage (e.g., 90% or 95%) of its final value in response to a step change in incident light intensity.

The choice of photodetector technology and design parameters can significantly impact the response time. Different types of photodetectors, such as avalanche photodiodes (APDs), photomultiplier tubes (PMTs), and solid-state photodiodes, have varying response times due to their distinct operating principles and structures.

It is important to consider the response time requirements for specific applications. For instance, high-speed optical communication systems and laser-based applications often require photodetectors with fast response times to accurately capture rapidly changing light signals. On the other hand, in applications where slower variations in light intensity are expected, response time may be less critical.

10. Noise

Noise:
In a communication system, noise refers to unwanted disturbances that can interfere with the received signal. In optical fiber communication systems, there are three main types of noise that can affect the performance: thermal noise, dark current noise, and quantum noise.

10.1. Thermal Noise:
Thermal noise arises from the random motion of electrons, which is present at any finite temperature.

The thermal noise current can be calculated using the formula:

it2 = 4KTBR

where:
K is Boltzmann's constant,
T is the absolute temperature,
B is the post-detection bandwidth of the system.
10.2. Dark Noise:
Even when there is no optical power incident on the photodetector, a small reverse leakage current called dark current still flows through the device terminals. This dark current contributes to the overall system noise and introduces random fluctuations around the average photocurrent flow.

The dark current noise can be expressed as:

id2 = 2eBId

where:
e is the charge on an electron,
B is the post-detection bandwidth of the system.
Id is the dark current.
10.3. Quantum Noise:
Quantum noise, also known as shot noise, is caused by the discrete nature of electrons. It arises from the statistical production and collection of photoelectrons. The quantum noise can be given by:

is2 = 2eBIp

where:
e is the charge on an electron,
B is the post-detection bandwidth of the system,
Ip is the photocurrent.

In addition to these types of noise, other factors such as amplifier noise, receiver noise, and environmental interference can also contribute to the overall noise in an optical fiber communication system.

Reducing noise and improving the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is crucial for achieving reliable and high-quality communication. Various techniques such as signal amplification, filtering, and advanced modulation schemes are employed to mitigate the impact of noise and enhance the performance of optical fiber communication systems.

11. Receiver Sensitivity

Receiver sensitivity in photodetectors refers to the minimum optical power level required for the photodetector to reliably detect and convert incoming light signals into electrical signals with a specified bit error rate (BER). It is a crucial parameter that determines the ability of a photodetector to detect weak optical signals and plays a key role in the overall performance of optical communication systems.

Receiver sensitivity is influenced by several factors. Firstly, the responsivity of the photodetector plays a significant role. Higher responsivity allows for stronger electrical signals to be generated for a given optical input, improving the receiver sensitivity. Additionally, noise sources, such as thermal noise, dark current noise, and shot noise, can degrade the receiver sensitivity. Minimizing noise through proper design, signal amplification, and noise reduction techniques helps improve the receiver sensitivity.

The bandwidth of the photodetector and the receiver circuitry also affect the receiver sensitivity. A wider bandwidth allows for the detection of a broader range of optical signals but may increase noise levels. Balancing the bandwidth with noise considerations is important for optimizing receiver sensitivity.

By achieving higher receiver sensitivity, the photodetector can detect weaker optical signals, leading to increased communication range, improved data transmission rates, and enhanced system performance. It enables reliable and accurate detection of optical signals in various applications such as optical fiber communications, remote sensing, and imaging systems.

12. Bit Error Rate

It is defined as the ratio of the number of bits received in error to the total number of bits transmitted.

BER=Ne/Nt

where,

Ne = the number of errors
Nt=total number of transmitted pulses during time ‘t’.
It is the probability of a bit being incorrectly received.

BER is expressed as a percentage or as a decimal number between 0 and 1.For example, a BER of 0.01means that for every 100 bits transmitted,one bit is received in error.

BER is affected by various factors such as noise,interference,attenuation and distortion in the communication channel. The lower the BER, the better the quality of transmission.

BER depends on SNR(signal to noise ratio)

Higher values of BER indicate degradation in the performance of the receiver.

The Bit Error Rate (BER) in a photodetector refers to the rate at which errors occur in the received electrical signal as a result of noise, distortion, and other impairments. It is a critical metric that quantifies the accuracy of data transmission and reception in communication systems utilizing photodetectors.

The BER is influenced by various factors related to the photodetector and the overall system. Noise sources such as thermal noise, dark current noise, and shot noise contribute to the occurrence of errors in the received signal. Minimizing noise through proper design, noise reduction techniques, and optimizing the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) helps to reduce the BER.
